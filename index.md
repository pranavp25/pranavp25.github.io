# Welcome

Hi, I'm Pranav Premnath, an astrophysics Ph.D. candidate at the University of California, Irvine, working in Dr. Paul Robertson's UCI Planet Hunters Group. My research focuses on discovering and characterizing giant exoplanets around M-dwarf stars while developing techniques to improve the precision of radial velocity measurements for the detection of Earth-like planets.

My primary research is with the **GEMS (Giant Exoplanets around M-dwarf Stars) Survey**, a volume-limited search for transiting giant planets orbiting nearby M dwarfs. Giant planets around these low-mass stars are intrinsically rare, making each new discovery an important test of planet formation theory. By expanding the known population and obtaining precise mass and radius measurements, we can investigate how giant planet occurrence depends on stellar mass, compare their internal structures with giant planets around Sun-like stars, and place stronger constraints on the processes that form planetary systems.

I also study how stellar surface features—--specifically  starspots---affect high-precision radial velocity measurements. Using high-resolution spectroscopy from instruments such as the Keck Planet Finder (KPF), I investigate how stellar activity introduces astrophysical noise into RV observations and develop methods to mitigate these effects, improving our ability to detect small, temperate exoplanets.

Before joining UC Irvine, I earned my B.S. in Astronomy and Physics with distinction in research from the University of Texas at Austin. My undergraduate research included dynamical mass measurements of young stars using ALMA observations and the identification of an obscured active galactic nucleus in a high-redshift galaxy, both published as first-author Research Notes. I also worked at the SETI Institute's Hat Creek Radio Observatory, contributing to searches for fast radio bursts, pulsars, and technosignatures.
